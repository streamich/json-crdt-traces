Yeah it kind of is.\n\nSo yeah, it was 4 hours a day. 2 hours of movement (I love this in theory because it's all about loosening up and limbering up and getting more into you physical body because that is your instrument - I can't believe in hindsight that other places don't do this too. On the other hand, in practice I didn't like this quite so much because my knees are a bit sus from a past life as a ballet dancer and a lot of the movement felt like Primary School games *cue the SFX of children's playground taunts flashback*). Yikes. Then the next 2 hours was Le Jeu (the game) or Clowning. But they'd crammed 25 people or so into this class because the teacher was this master who'd been\naround for 40 years, so the appetite was here. And I don't know if this is a French thing but they didn't really bother to make sure \"everybody got a turn.\" And again, I will repeat what is becoming a kind of catchcry of mine about this whole experience. \"I actually kind of get what they were going for. It makes sense. But it also really hurt.\" Sitting for 2 hours watching other people metaphorically elbow each other in the face to try to get some stagetime in front of the master himself put me in this wildly frustrating bind - I absolutely did not want to feel like I'd sold out my morals instantly to try to push other people out of the way. But then again, the same people who were more unabashed about just taking their space on stage would keep reappearing and I'd spend two hours sitting in a chair, watching them flop on stage (as we all did - we all got less funny as time went on but will explain that later on). Sitting for 2 hours made you lose momentum and inspiration, and I felt myself shrinking and a wall coming up, trying to make it ok. \"It's fine. I'm ok with this. I won't elbow anyone in the ribs just to get some time on stage. I'll watch and listen and observe and take great notes.\" But it didn't feel ok. It felt like being complicit with getting pushed around by an arbitrary number of people in the class who'd sel-elected as being the ones who deserved more stagetime than anyone. It was a huge distraction. But I can see some benefit, even in a weird way. You kind of are going to have to learn to beleive in yourself and have this healthy almost singular drive to get on stage and *dazzle* the audience. No time like now to get that competitive edge (I am the baby of the family, and so my competitive drive never really developed. Who cares what others are doing? They're all older and smarter. I'm way more interested in my own thing, learning and improving on my own terms).\n\nIn comparison, I loved movement class. I felt so much more fit by the end. I've barely been exercising for the last few years and doing 2 hours every day was great.\n\nBut the broader context of that was le jeu. The Game. So, how do I describe it? Mmmm take a tennis ball and put it between your head and someone else's head, so you can look each other in the eyes. Then play with each other. Move so that the ball doesn't fall down. Don't try to \"win\" too hard. The goal is to make your partner laugh and to enjoy yourself. One of the best exercises we did regularly in movement class was stuff like that, and we'd all do it at the same time in pairs. Then a few pairs would get up and perform in front of everyone. It was obvious who we wanted to see more of, as the audience. And we'd point them out and try to name why we liked them a bit. But a lot of it was instinctive. \"Those two were boring. Those two were really funny for some reason\". We didn't all agree as a group - someone would love a couple for no good reason, and everyone else is like ???? you're cracked. But yeah, over and over again. Is it working yet? Was it fun? Am I doing \"fun\" right? (No, you're not).\n\nAnd then there's The Flop. Sooo, if you're on stage and you're boring and everyone hates you, we want you to get off stage. A lot of clowning was learning to become sensitive to the flop. And learning to acknowledge it. The audience knows when you flop. You'd think they'd want you to leave, or feel ashamed or something. But we don't. We want the clown to acknowledge it - everyone's acknowledgement is different. And then try something else. The teacher held a drum, and when it was beaten 3 times, thats it - you're out. \"Come with us\".\n\nThere's a book called \"The courage to be disliked\". In it they say that basically every problem in our lives comes down to a fear of being disliked by our peers. And there's something weirdly powerful about actually stepping into that particular fire pit and getting burned by it over and over again. It hurts. But the parts which burn away are the parts which can't do it. Can't face down those demons.\n\nI got one piece of feedback that I keep coming back to - something along the lines of kind of not liking or even kind of having a distaste for the audience heh heh is it that obvious? All the teaching staff were exquisitely sensitive, which is an odd thing to say when someone is telling you you're a terrible student or deeply unfunny. But the things they pointed at are real. I kind of don't need the audience, don't need their validation and I don't respect the things they cheer for (I just need to see what's trending on Audible - good GRIEF you have the entire world's collection of words and a limited time on earth and you are reading THAT celebrity ghostwritten memoir??? Really???) I had this experience of playing the *new* Guitar Hero back in the day and there were all these groupies in the front row, chanting and loving your songs and I just wanted to tell them to calm down and just chill. This seems to be the opposite to what a lot of celebrities and performers have - an irrationally strong drive to be loved by strangers. I think I kind of create my own world that's beautiful and adventurous and full of mystery and endlessly interesting, and then the spell is broken quickly and the dream fades as soon as I try to explain it to someone. \"Oh, that's just like this story I read, where .....\" And it kind of feels like it undermines the splendour, quickly dismisses it as just another thing. I don't really want to share it, unless of course someone else wanted to share that worldview, leap into it, look around, try it, lick the walls. #IntrovertDilemmas. I don't demand you like it. But I would want you to know what it is before you dismiss it. So yeah, clown school is where I learnt that, if you're sensitive (and maybe even if you're not), this is written on my face.\n\nThis is the thing. It was traumatic. We really do have clown based trauma. And, we're so much looser. I can't describe the looseness - like, I was in a lift earlier today and this girl got in - maybe 28 years old. We were chatting and she was half involved / listening to our conversation. It was kind of awkward. A few years ago I would have felt awful and so awkward - \"oh no, what did she think of us??\". Now, I just looked at her and said something - I can't remember what - in an attempt to acknowledge the conversation. She gave a weird smile then got out at her floor and that was that. It wasn't the best show on earth, but it was fiiine. There's nothing I'm holding on to after that. I think there's this quality thats hard to name where before I did clowning, I found it too overwhelming to really be present with how people saw me. If I thought about it too much I'd want to run away and dissociate. It was just too painful to face up to the reality that I'm not perfect. I'm flawed, and weak, and deeply unfunny and kind of disgusting and weird. And I couldn't bear to see myself reflected in that light. Clowning was 4 weeks of exposure therapy of being present with how boring we are. How we have nothing. And now, after having the truth of that perspective shoved in my face, in front of a room full of our peers over and over again... I dunno, something feels lighter. Being a fool in front of everyone is much easier - but so is everything else. The worst thing that'll happen is what - they'll hate me? Want their money back? So? And the best thing that happens is we connect - which was never quite possible so long as I was playing a con on them. I wanted something - approval, not rejection, something. We know. We can smell that desperation on others. Its boring and unattractive. Its disconnecting.\n\nThese words I'm using? They're familiar to me after clowning because thats what a lot of the feedback was like, and what it was about. Basically different versions of \"you're shit. Get off stage.\". On one of the first days I was told that I was so deeply unattractive on stage that the teacher now understood why people of the opposite sex become gay. Someone else was told that the teacher wishes she died by being put in a big rubbish bin (\"trash can\") full of rotting fish until she suffocated. I won't go through all the (fantastic) sledges issued by the teacher. But it was all forms of \"You aren't here with us. You're dissociating and we don't feel like you even like us and we hate you because of it\". And, I know exactly what the teachers were getting at with all of that. 4 weeks in and they're right! Someone got called a cancer on stage and I know what they meant. Its terrible feedback - how do you improve with that? But maybe it wasn't for the performer. I dunno.\n\nI wrote down some of the advice they gave us in clown school one day when I was feeling upset and like nothing I did worked. Stop being so conventional. Be sensitive. Look at us - show us your eyes. Come on stage with a dream. Stop bringing your shitty ideas on stage. Find your impulse! This is all impossible to do in some sense. Its in the subtle realm. But its fucking fantastic advice. The first exercise we did was one of my favorites. They got us to stand up in front of everyone and imagine we're in a giant stadium with thousands of people. And we'd come on stage (one by one) and say \"Hi mum! Hi dad! I'm in Etampes! I'm going to sing you a song!\" and then sing a song or say a poem we know or something. And when the flop happens - when everyone in the room gets bored of you and wants you to get off stage, notice it and acknowledge it. Well, I can tell you for sure that I watched a room full of 25 people die that day. One by one. Each in our own beautiful, sad ways. I kind of ran away once I flopped. Sagged into a ball and disappeared. Some people tried to pretend they weren't flopping. Or tried singing another song. (We weren't buying it). Some people physically fled. Or apologised. Most of us looked down. But - show us your eyes. We miss you. Its the most remarkable thing, being both on stage and in the audience. In the audience its so obvious what we want. We want to see the performers eyes. We want to connect with them over the very thing that they're struggling with. When we get bored of their boring shitty performance, we want to see the real them shine through. Except, when you're on stage and you flop, being seen is the last thing in the world you want. You just want to go and die in shame.\n\nThere's the heart of clowning, right there. Both of those things. Sensitivity to the audience. Connection. Acknowledging the flop. And being yourself. And play - finding something fun that lights you up. The most beautiful thing in the world is watching someone's eyes light up with the sparkle of having a dream, of something they're in love with in that moment. Something they want to share. It doesn't matter if it fails on stage. We're there with the performer. We don't care if they succeed. We want them to *try*.\n\nThey kept coming back to sensitivity constantly, and this already is quite an unusual thing for me - being \"sensitive\" was something I heard maybe every day or two, and it seemed to be code for \"oversensitive\" or being a wimp or a wuss or some other kind of 80s Australian slang for feeling things too deeply when the world seemed to want you to not care, be cool, not try, fit in, cruise through and not ever be too interested in anything. This seemed a bit shallow, not as a slight but as a literal observation - like trailing your fingers in some water and calling it \"swimming.\" So to hear that sensitivity on stage to the audience is an asset, well it was strangely refeshing. Go deep. Get your clothes wet. Go in too deep and flounder, and it might feel like you're drowning but you survive. Your ego takes a massive hit. You feel shame, good god do you feel shame, inescapable shame that you are sure that everyone saw and instead it barely rates a blip on the day's ECG of events. And you may turn up the day after (or the day after that, because you may sit at home, giving yourself some reprieve while eating your body weight in French boulangerie treats - almost a cliche, but a cliche for a DAMN good reason). But at some point, you go back to clown school, put on your sweaty tracksuit pants, and you realise you were basically cancelled in realtime on stage on Tuesday, but on Thursday you made the French master clown and his wife laugh and you know that there are no easy wins or token words of encouragement in French clown school and it feels amazing. Just another day. You survived. People still want to be your friend. You weren't rejected from the tribe. \n\nAnd thats it. You don't get rejected for trying something. For taking a risk and having it go well or badly. Really, you get rejected for the opposite. For being small. For being insensitive to the audience, and not connecting with them. You get rejected implicitly when you take yourself out of the running because you're too scared to be seen.\n\nWe want to be dazzled. We want people to connect with us. To try to connect with us. We want to feel like we're worth being impressed by the performers. Its fun. It brings us alive. We want people to take risks on stage.\n\nMaybe, just maybe, it's ok to take a few more risks in real life too. Depends on where your baseline started, of course. There are no easy rules - go more left, be more free. There is just increasing discernment, as someone wise once told me.\n\nWait, what's that?\n\n*low chanting* money back\n\nWhat???\n\n*GETTING LOUDER NOW* MONEY BACK!
